# 🌍 Climate Change Indicators: Trends and Correlations

> [cite_start]**Project Overview:** > This report explores the relationship between greenhouse gas emissions (CO₂) and various socio-economic/environmental indicators across different income groups using World Bank data[cite: 5, 6].

---

### 📊 Key Analysis Summary

| Feature | Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Indicators** | [cite_start]CO₂ emissions, GDP per capita, Renewable energy, Urban population, Forest area[cite: 20, 21, 22, 23, 24]. |
| **Income Groups** | [cite_start]High (Germany, Saudi Arabia), Middle (Brazil, Nigeria), Low (Angola, Bangladesh)[cite: 27, 28, 29]. |
| **Time Intervals** | [cite_start]Early (1980-2000) and Recent (2000-2020)[cite: 6, 31]. |
| **Methodology** | [cite_start]Pearson Correlation and Bootstrapping (10,000 resamples)[cite: 34, 61]. |

---

### 🔍 Major Findings

* [cite_start]**Decoupling in High-Income Nations:** In countries like Germany, the correlation between GDP and emissions dropped significantly (from 0.85 to 0.32), suggesting economic growth is becoming less dependent on carbon[cite: 37, 62, 65].
* [cite_start]**Rising Trends in Low-Income Nations:** Bangladesh and Angola show increasing rates of CO₂ change as their economic growth remains carbon-intensive[cite: 40, 83].
* [cite_start]**Renewable Energy Growth:** A notable rise in renewable energy share was observed in Germany and Brazil between 2000-2020[cite: 66, 68].
* [cite_start]**Statistical Significance:** Bootstrapping confirms that the observed changes in trends and correlations are statistically significant[cite: 61, 87].
